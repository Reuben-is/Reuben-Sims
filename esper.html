The Brief

What problem is worth solving?

ADHD affects the brain's executive functioning — attention, motivation, and the ability to regulate behaviour. It impacts roughly 7% of people globally. The challenge was to design something that genuinely supports day-to-day life for people who struggle, without being patronising or clinical.

ESPER is a standalone physical device that supports ADHD through gamified task management — part companion, part reminder system, part motivator.

Research

"I can't make myself do the task, just like someone can't make themselves touch a hot stove."

— ADHD interview respondent

Ethnographic interviews

User interviews surfaced three core pain points that ESPER would need to address: motivation collapse, decision paralysis, and poor short-term memory. Understanding these wasn't just about the symptoms — it was about how they felt and what had already been tried.

A user persona, Imogen Gray, was developed based on interview synthesis to ground design decisions throughout the project.

User persona / interview image

Motivation

Tasks feel impossible even when the person wants to do them. Not laziness — a neurological barrier.

Indecision

Too many open tasks create paralysis. Users need help prioritising, not just tracking.

Memory

Out of sight, out of mind. Physical, ambient reminders beat screen notifications.

Ideation

Concept Sketching

Digital Assistant

Early sketches explored wearable tech, clip-on dongles, and companion devices. The concept evolved toward a reverse Tamagotchi — a companion that prompts tasks, sets reminders, and responds to the user's presence rather than demanding attention.

Think: third-party task organisation that lives on your desk and has personality.

Development
1

CAD Modelling — Fusion 360

The housing was designed in Fusion 360, iterating through form studies to land on a compact, handheld device with a friendly, slightly toy-like quality. The shape needed to feel approachable, not clinical.

2

Electronics — Arduino

An Arduino-based prototype handled the interaction logic. The device swaps between screens, responds to input, and runs a gamified task management flow. Hardware was selected for its accessibility and ability to demo quickly.

3

UI Design — Figma

The screen UI was designed in Figma and loaded via an LCD module. While the prototype could only swap between static screens, it communicated the interaction vision clearly to stakeholders and judges.

4

Rendering — Keyshot

Final product renders in Keyshot explored colourways and material finishes, communicating the visual language of the product beyond the physical prototype.

The result

The final prototype was an Arduino-based device housed in a 3D-printed enclosure with an LCD screen. While non-functional beyond screen transitions, it successfully communicated the concept to judges and audience members at Monash's MADA NOW exhibition.

The prototype was a massive success — it allowed for clear communication of the concept and validated the design direction through real audience interaction.
