The Studio

A one-person studio run purely on passion.

Matchbox is a small jewellery studio — by Reuben, for people who want something made by hand with intention. It started on Instagram as a way to make unique artifacts beyond the constraints of university briefs, and grew into a commission-based practice.

Each piece is handmade in silversmithing — fabricated, filed, formed, and finished by hand. No casting from a mould. Every piece is negotiated with the client, scoped carefully, and made to last.

Making things is how I think. Matchbox is where I go when I want to make something with no brief, no deadline, and no compromise.

Process
1

Consultation & Scoping

Every commission starts with a conversation. Understanding what the client wants, what they value, what the piece needs to mean — this is design thinking applied to something small and personal.

2

Sketching & Planning

Rough sketches establish form before touching metal. Material selection — silver weight, gauge, finishing method — is decided at this stage based on the brief and budget.

3

Fabrication

Sheet silver is cut, shaped, soldered, and refined by hand using traditional silversmithing tools. Each join is carefully made and cleaned. No shortcuts — the work shows.

4

Finishing

Filing, sanding, polishing — the finish is everything in jewellery. Whether mirror-bright or matte brushed, the final surface reflects the character of the piece and the taste of the wearer.

What it means

Design beyond the brief

Matchbox is where theory becomes practice in the most direct sense. Every skill used in industrial design — material understanding, form, function, user research, negotiation, scoping — gets exercised at a small, personal scale.

Running a studio also develops business literacy: pricing work fairly, managing client expectations, delivering on time, and building a reputation through quality.

Skills practiced

Silversmithing, fabrication, client consultation, pricing, Instagram content, web presence.

What it proves

That making things with your hands and running a business are both design problems — and I'm interested in both.
